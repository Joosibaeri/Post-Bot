3 things I learned while building a tool that writes LinkedIn posts for me—yes, the irony isn’t lost on me. 🎨 There’s something humbling about teaching a machine to sound human when I’m still figuring out how to do that myself.

This latest push to Post-Bot (now at 144 commits—check it out here: https://github.com/cliff-de-tech/Post-Bot) was all about refining the voice engine. I wanted the output to feel less like a robot and more like a peer sharing real insights, not just buzzwords. Turns out, the secret isn’t just better prompts—it’s layering in constraints. Things like forcing shorter paragraphs, banning corporate jargon, and even adding a "vibe check" to reject anything that sounds like a generic thought leader. The biggest lesson? Creativity thrives in boundaries, not blank slates. 🚀

What surprised me was how much this mirrored my own writing process. I’ve spent years overcomplicating my posts, thinking more words = more value. But the best conversations happen in the gaps between sentences. That’s where trust is built, where ideas actually land. This project forced me to distill my own voice into rules a machine could follow—and in doing so, I realized I’d been breaking my own rules all along. ✨

Here’s the thing: tools like this don’t replace human creativity—they reveal it. By automating the repetitive parts (formatting, hashtag trends, basic structure), I’m free to focus on what actually matters: the why behind the words. That’s where the real connection happens. And if a bot can teach me that, imagine what it could do for your workflow.
